# 01 — Regulations & Certification (U.S. Aviation Maintenance)

> How you become legally authorized to work on aircraft in the United States, and the
> rules that govern every maintenance action. All primary sources below are **free**
> U.S. Government publications.

---

## 1. The A&P Mechanic Certificate

In the U.S., the core credential is the **FAA Mechanic Certificate** with **Airframe (A)**
and **Powerplant (P)** ratings — commonly called an **"A&P."** It is issued under
**14 CFR Part 65, Subpart D.** [[E1]](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-65)

### Eligibility (Part 65.71–65.77)
- Be **at least 18** years old.
- Be able to **read, write, speak, and understand English**.
- Meet the **experience** requirement by ONE of two routes:
  - **Route A – Practical experience:** 18 months of documented experience for a single
    rating (A *or* P), **or 30 months** working on both airframes and powerplants for the
    combined A&P.
  - **Route B – Part 147 AMT School:** Graduate from an **FAA-certificated Aviation
    Maintenance Technician School** (~1,900 curriculum hours). [[E2]](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-H/part-147)

### The three-part testing gauntlet (per rating)
1. **Written (knowledge) tests** — computer-based multiple choice:
   - **General** (AMG) — required once; covers material shared by both ratings.
   - **Airframe** (AMA) and/or **Powerplant** (AMP).
2. **Oral test** — a Designated Mechanic Examiner (DME) questions you on each subject area.
3. **Practical test** — you demonstrate hands-on tasks (safety wiring, riveting, mag
   timing, inspection, records, etc.).

Passing all three for both ratings earns the combined **A&P**. Add-on ratings like
**Inspection Authorization (IA)** come later (Part 65.91) after **3 years as an A&P** and
allow approving major repairs/alterations and performing annual inspections.

## 2. The Core Regulations (14 CFR — free on eCFR.gov)

| Part | Title | What it governs | Link |
|---|---|---|---|
| **43** | Maintenance, Preventive Maintenance, Rebuilding, and Alteration | The "how" of every maintenance action, who may perform it, required records, return-to-service | [[E3]](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-C/part-43) |
| **65** | Certification: Airmen Other Than Flight Crewmembers | Mechanic & repairman certificates, privileges, limitations, IA | [[E1]](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-65) |
| **91** | General Operating and Flight Rules | Owner/operator maintenance responsibilities, inspection requirements (annual/100-hr), ADs | [[E4]](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-F/part-91) |
| **145** | Repair Stations | Certificated repair-station operations & quality systems | [[E5]](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-H/part-145) |
| **147** | Aviation Maintenance Technician Schools | AMT school curriculum & certification | [[E2]](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-I/subchapter-H/part-147) |

**Key Part 43 concepts every mechanic must know:**
- **§43.3** — Persons authorized to perform maintenance.
- **§43.7** — Persons authorized to *approve for return to service*.
- **§43.9 / §43.11** — Content of **maintenance records** (the logbook entry) and
  **inspection** entries. A compliant entry states: description of work, date completed,
  name of person, and signature + certificate number of the person approving return to service.
- **§43.13** — Performance rules ("general") — do the work using methods acceptable to
  the Administrator (this is where **AC 43.13** comes in).
- **Appendix A** — Major vs. minor alterations/repairs.
- **Appendix B** — Form 337 recording of major repairs/alterations.
- **Appendix D** — Scope & detail of an **annual/100-hour inspection**.

**Preventive maintenance** (Part 43 Appendix A(c)) is a defined list of simple tasks a
**certificated pilot** may perform on their own aircraft (e.g., oil change, tire, spark
plugs) — a useful boundary line for mechanics to know.

## 3. Advisory Circulars (FAA "acceptable methods" — free PDFs)

Advisory Circulars (ACs) are non-regulatory but describe **methods the FAA accepts**.
The two bibles of hands-on practice:

- **AC 43.13-1B — Acceptable Methods, Techniques, and Practices: Aircraft Inspection &
  Repair.** Structural repair, wood/fabric, welding, fasteners, electrical, corrosion.
  This is the go-to reference when no manufacturer data exists. [[E6]](https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/advisory_circulars)
- **AC 43.13-2B — Acceptable Methods…: Aircraft Alterations.** Antenna installs, avionics,
  cargo, skis, floats, structural mods.
- **AC 65-series** — mechanic certification & test guidance.
- **AC 20-series** — general airworthiness (e.g., AC 20-62 parts eligibility, AC 20-125
  corrosion). Search the full library at the FAA AC portal. [[E6]](https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/advisory_circulars)

## 4. Airworthiness & Continuing-Maintenance Documents

- **Airworthiness Directives (ADs)** — *legally mandatory* fixes for unsafe conditions on
  a type of aircraft/engine/part. Issued under **14 CFR Part 39**. Free, searchable in the
  **Dynamic Regulatory System (DRS)**. [[E7]](https://drs.faa.gov/)
- **Service Bulletins (SBs) / Service Letters** — manufacturer recommendations
  (mandatory only if referenced by an AD or ops spec).
- **Type Certificate Data Sheets (TCDS)** — the aircraft/engine's "birth certificate":
  approved configurations, limits, part numbers. Free in DRS. [[E7]](https://drs.faa.gov/)
- **STCs (Supplemental Type Certificates)** — approved modifications to a type design.
- **ICA (Instructions for Continued Airworthiness)** — the maintenance manual data the
  manufacturer must provide.

**Records rule of thumb:** *If it isn't documented, it didn't happen.* Every action needs
a §43.9-compliant entry, and the aircraft's permanent records must show current status of
ADs, life-limited parts, and the last required inspections.

## 5. Primary Free Study Sources (the "textbooks")

The FAA publishes the entire A&P knowledge base for **free** — the three-volume
**Aviation Maintenance Technician Handbook (AMTH)** series, on the FAA Handbooks page:
[[E8]](https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/handbooks_manuals/aviation)

| Handbook | Doc # | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| AMTH – **General** | FAA-H-8083-30B (2023) | Math, physics, materials, electricity, tools, inspection, safety, records |
| AMTH – **Airframe** | FAA-H-8083-31B (2023) | Structures, systems, sheet metal, hydraulics, electrical, avionics |
| AMTH – **Powerplant** | FAA-H-8083-32B (2023) | Reciprocating & turbine engines, fuel, ignition, props |

Also useful & free: **Airplane Flying Handbook (FAA-H-8083-3C)**, **Pilot's Handbook of
Aeronautical Knowledge (FAA-H-8083-25C)**, and the **Weight & Balance Handbook
(FAA-H-8083-1B)** — all on the same page. [[E8]](https://www.faa.gov/regulations_policies/handbooks_manuals/aviation)

## 6. International Note (EASA)

Outside the U.S., **EASA Part-66** licenses (categories **A, B1 mechanical, B2 avionics,
C**) are the equivalent, with training standards under **Part-147**. Concepts map closely
to the FAA system but the legal framework differs. EASA regulations are published free at
easa.europa.eu.
